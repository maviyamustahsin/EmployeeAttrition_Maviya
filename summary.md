# Employee Attrition Analysis — Executive Summary
### Prepared for: HR Director | Date: June 2026
### Analyst: Maviya

---

## Why This Report Matters

Every time an employee leaves, the company spends significant time and money finding, hiring, and training a replacement. This report uses data from 1,470 employees to identify **who is most likely to leave and why** — so that HR can take action *before* losing valuable team members.

---

## Key Finding #1: 16% of Employees Left the Company

Out of 1,470 employees studied, **237 (16.1%) left the company**. While this may seem manageable, the cost adds up quickly — and certain groups are far more affected than others.

---

## Key Finding #2: Sales Representatives Are at the Highest Risk

Nearly **4 out of every 10 Sales Representatives** left the company (39.8% attrition rate). This is by far the highest exit rate across all roles. For comparison, only 2.5% of Research Directors and 4.9% of Managers left.

**By department**, Sales (20.6%) and Human Resources (19.0%) lost more employees than Research & Development (13.8%).

---

## Key Finding #3: It's Not Just About Money

Employees who left earned approximately **$2,000 less per month** than those who stayed ($4,787 vs $6,833 average). However, salary alone does not explain why people leave. Our analysis found three factors that matter even more:

1. **Overtime** — Employees who regularly work overtime are nearly **three times more likely to leave** (30.5% vs 10.4%). This is the single strongest warning sign.
2. **Frequent Business Travel** — Employees who travel frequently for work leave at much higher rates than those who rarely or never travel.
3. **Job Role and Early Tenure** — Employees in certain roles (Sales Representatives, Lab Technicians) and those in their **first year** at the company are most vulnerable to leaving.

---

## What HR Can Do — Two Actionable Recommendations

### Recommendation 1: Create an "Overtime Watch" Program
Since overtime is the #1 predictor of employees leaving, HR should:
- Flag employees consistently working overtime for more than 3 months
- Schedule check-in conversations with their managers
- Review staffing levels in departments with chronic overtime (especially Sales)
- Offer compensatory time off or flexible scheduling options

### Recommendation 2: Launch a "First Year Retention" Initiative for Sales
With nearly 40% of Sales Representatives leaving and most attrition occurring in the first year:
- Assign a senior mentor to every new Sales hire for their first 12 months
- Conduct structured 30/60/90-day check-ins focused on job satisfaction
- Review the commission and target structure for junior Sales roles
- Provide a clear promotion pathway so new hires can see their future at the company

---

## Important Caveat

This analysis identifies **patterns and risk factors**, not guarantees. Not every employee flagged as "at risk" will actually leave, and some who leave may not show any warning signs. This model should be used as **one tool among many** to guide supportive conversations — not as a surveillance mechanism or a basis for negative employment decisions.

---

## How the Analysis Was Done (In Brief)

We used a statistical model trained on employee data (demographics, job characteristics, satisfaction scores, and compensation) to predict who is likely to leave. The model correctly identifies at-risk employees with approximately **80% reliability** when tested on unseen data. Three different modeling approaches were compared, and the most reliable one was selected for the findings above.
